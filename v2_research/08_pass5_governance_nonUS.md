## Competency 5 — Governance architecture for deployment (the success side)

### Open University "Ethical Use of Student Data" — Designing Consent to Unblock Analytics
- proposed-id: v2-501
- year: 2014 (intervention evaluated 2016–2025)
- scale: big
- proposed-kind: intervention
- proposed-anchor: 5.1 — Stakeholder trust and consent as preconditions
- alternate-anchors: [5.4 — Change-control and disclosure as governance artifacts; 8.3 — Designing predictions to trigger support, not gatekeeping]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: Slade & Prinsloo, "Learning Analytics: Ethical Issues and Dilemmas," American Behavioral Scientist 2013, DOI 10.1177/0002764213479366 (verified); OU policy (first of its kind in HE, 2014); intervention evidence: Herodotou et al., "Analytics4Action," JIME 2016, DOI 10.5334/jime.394 (verified); OU Analyse evaluation, 559 teachers / 14,000+ students (2025, verified)
- domains: higher education (UK), learning analytics, data governance
- capability question: Could a university deploy predictive analytics on student data without violating student trust — and would the resulting interventions actually help students?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: Predictive learning analytics raised a real governance objection at the Open University: large-scale processing of student data for interventions, under pre-GDPR scrutiny, with genuine surveillance concerns. Rather than abandon the program or push it through, the OU in 2014 produced the first institutional "Ethical Use of Student Data for Learning Analytics" policy in higher education — eight principles built through wide stakeholder consultation including students, framing students as participants rather than data subjects. This consent-and-transparency architecture is what allowed OU Analyse (a weekly machine-learning at-risk prediction system) to operate; the Analytics4Action framework then paired predictions with tutor judgment and documented interventions on modules of 3,000+ students. A 2025 evaluation across 559 teachers and 14,000+ students examined how degree of system usage related to outcomes. The honest open question, which the OU's own researchers raise: whether predictive analytics genuinely serves students versus surveils them remains contested, and the policy has since been superseded by a broader Data Ethics Policy — governance as a living artifact, not a solved problem.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: This is the cleanest case in the sweep of the governance objection being overcome *by design* — the consent framework was the enabling engineering, not a compliance afterthought, and the deployment followed because trust was established first.

### LALA — Building Learning-Analytics Governance Capacity Across Latin America
- proposed-id: v2-502
- year: 2017–2020
- scale: small
- proposed-kind: intervention
- proposed-anchor: 5.1 — Stakeholder trust and consent as preconditions
- alternate-anchors: [5.3 — Cross-regime and platform governance seams; 6.4 — Cross-domain adaptation of proven interventions]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: Hilliger et al., "Identifying needs for learning analytics adoption in Latin American universities: A mixed-methods approach," The Internet and Higher Education 2020, DOI 10.1016/j.iheduc.2020.100726 (verified); LALA project (EU grant 586120-EPP-1-2017-1-ES)
- domains: higher education (Chile, Ecuador, Mexico), learning analytics, cross-national governance
- capability question: Could learning analytics be adopted in Latin American universities in a way that fit local governance, data-literacy, and trust conditions rather than importing a Global-North template?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: The LALA project explicitly rejected lifting US/European learning-analytics tools wholesale, recognizing that adoption fails if not integrated with local learning design and institutional decision-making. Through structured interviews with administrators and focus groups with students and teachers across Chilean, Ecuadorian, and Mexican universities, the team built a participatory adoption model (the LALA CANVAS) and identified that stakeholders wanted data-driven feedback *with* ethical responsibility as a precondition, not despite it. The honest limit: this is adoption-readiness and capacity-building evidence, not yet long-run outcome evidence that the deployed systems improved retention; the contribution is the participatory governance method that made adoption locally legitimate.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: It treats stakeholder trust and local-context consent as the precondition for deployment, and engineers a participatory method to establish it — a non-US model for overcoming the adoption/governance objection.

### Data Privacy and Learning Analytics on the African Continent
- proposed-id: v2-503
- year: 2022
- scale: small
- proposed-kind: frontier
- proposed-anchor: 5.3 — Cross-regime and platform governance seams
- alternate-anchors: [5.1 — Stakeholder trust and consent; 8.4 — Surfacing bias/limits through governance]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: Prinsloo et al., "Data privacy on the African continent: Opportunities, challenges and implications for learning analytics," British Journal of Educational Technology 2022, DOI 10.1111/bjet.13226 (verified)
- domains: higher education (Africa, multi-country), data privacy law, learning analytics
- capability question: Can African universities adopt learning analytics responsibly when most rely on platforms and analytic services hosted outside the continent, under fragmented and emerging data-privacy regimes?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: The paper maps the legal and regulatory privacy landscape across African jurisdictions and finds a growing trend toward comprehensive data-protection legislation, though few frameworks are yet enacted and cross-border data-transfer policies differ sharply between countries. The core governance seam: African higher education frequently uses learning platforms and analytics hosted by external providers, creating a cross-regime governance gap where student data crosses jurisdictions with inconsistent protection. The authors recommend common cross-border data-sharing frameworks. This is a frontier case — it documents the governance architecture needed for responsible adoption rather than a completed success — and it is valuable precisely because it surfaces a seam (extraterritorial platform hosting) the US-centric canon never confronts.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: It is squarely about cross-regime and platform governance seams — the under-examined 5.3 sub-competency — from a non-Western vantage.
- (frontier note) Points to a governance sub-problem the framework underweights: **capability deployment dependent on extraterritorial platforms**, where the governing regime and the operating regime differ. Increasingly common as institutions everywhere build on cloud/AI services they do not control.

### Norway's National Expert Commission on Learning Analytics
- proposed-id: v2-504
- year: 2022–2023
- scale: big
- proposed-kind: intervention
- proposed-anchor: 5.4 — Change-control and disclosure as governance artifacts
- alternate-anchors: [5.1 — Stakeholder trust and consent; 5.2 — Human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions]
- evidence-source: investigation
- citation: Norwegian Expert Commission on Learning Analytics, interim report to the Minister of Education and Research (June 2022); final report 2023 (NOU); summarized in Misiejuk & Wasson and in the JLA/practicable-analytics literature (verified via arXiv 2301.13043 and secondary academic sources)
- domains: education (Norway, national policy), learning analytics, governmental governance
- capability question: Should a country regulate and promote learning analytics across its entire education sector, and on what pedagogical, ethical, legal, and privacy basis?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: Rather than let learning analytics diffuse unregulated or block it, Norway's Ministry of Education convened a national Expert Commission to investigate the pedagogical, ethical, legal, and privacy issues and establish a regulatory foundation before sector-wide deployment. The commission delivered an interim report (June 2022) and a final report identifying central dilemmas (described in the literature as four core tensions). This is governance-as-deliberate-artifact at national scale — a country treating the change-control and disclosure question as a precondition for adoption. The open element: the commission's recommendations were guidance to a ministry, and downstream sector outcomes are not yet documented, so this is a governance-process success, not yet a measured deployment success.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: A national government produced an explicit governance artifact (commission, reports, dilemma framing) to enable responsible deployment — change-control and disclosure as the deliverable.

### MMALA — A Maturity Model for Responsible LA Adoption (Brazil)
- proposed-id: v2-505
- year: 2024
- scale: small
- proposed-kind: intervention
- proposed-anchor: 5.4 — Change-control and disclosure as governance artifacts
- alternate-anchors: [2.3 — Closed-loop evidence; 6.4 — Cross-domain adaptation]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: Freitas et al., "MMALA: Developing and Evaluating a Maturity Model for Adopting Learning Analytics," Journal of Learning Analytics 2024, 11(1):67–86 (verified)
- domains: higher education (Brazil), learning analytics, institutional governance
- capability question: Can institutions self-assess their readiness — technological, human, ethical, pedagogical — to adopt learning analytics responsibly, rather than deploying blind?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: MMALA is a maturity model spanning infrastructure, human resources, ethics, and pedagogy; experts evaluated it as comprehensive and suitable, and a validation exercise at three Brazilian universities found it could outline essential practices and support self-assessment for scaling. The honest limit: it is validated by expert opinion and a three-institution exercise, not by longitudinal outcomes of institutions that used it to adopt LA — it is an instrument for responsible adoption, with the downstream effect still to be measured.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: It is a governance artifact (a structured self-assessment for responsible deployment) that builds the ethics and disclosure check into the adoption process from the start.

## What this pass changes
1. **C5 is no longer failure-only.** The Open University case in particular is a clean, well-evidenced instance of learning-engineering principles (participatory consent design, evidence-linked intervention) *overcoming* a real governance objection and producing a deployed, evaluated system. That single case does the most to correct the "governance always fails" read; the other four thicken it across regimes.
2. **Non-US coverage is materially improved.** Five cases spanning the UK, Chile/Ecuador/Mexico, multi-country Africa, Norway, and Brazil. The v1 canon is heavily Anglo-American and physical-domain; this pass pushes into education governance across five non-US jurisdictions.
3. **A pattern worth naming in the book:** the documented "learning engineering overcame governance objection" cases cluster in education/learning-analytics, not in the high-consequence physical domains. One honest reading: in physical-safety domains governance objections are usually *correct* (the thing was unsafe), whereas in learning analytics the objection is often about trust and consent, which good design can genuinely resolve. That distinction is itself a teachable point about when governance is a barrier to route around versus a signal to heed.
4. **A recurring frontier seam:** extraterritorial-platform governance (v2-503) — capability deployed on platforms governed by a different regime than the one operating them. Increasingly universal as everyone builds on cloud/AI services they do not control; candidate C5 sub-competency.

## Caveats
- Three of five (LALA, Norway, MMALA) are governance-*process* or readiness successes, not yet measured deployment-outcome successes. Draft them as "the governance method worked," not "the deployment improved outcomes," unless the outcome evidence is added.
- The Open University case is the only one here with both a verified governance artifact AND verified downstream intervention evaluation; it should anchor the C5-success set.
- This pass leans entirely on the education/learning-analytics literature because that is where the requested pattern is genuinely documented. If the book wants a non-education C5 success (e.g., a medical-device or aviation governance objection overcome by evidence), that is a separate, harder search — flagged for a possible pass 6.
