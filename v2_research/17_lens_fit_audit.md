# LENS-Fit Audit — Do the 202 Cases Genuinely Teach Capability Engineering?

*Assessment only. No case files edited. Auditor brief: flag genuine weak fits, don't rubber-stamp.*

The test applied to each case: does it teach a **capability-engineering** lesson — whether people (or human-machine teams) inside a system can do what the system requires, and how that capability is designed, measured, trained, governed, or sustained — and does it map to a LENS/induced competency *without a stretch*, with a lens-approach that names a real learning-engineering teaching point? A case rates WEAK or POOR when it is really about something else (materials failure, financial fraud, pure regulatory/legal outcome, pure engineering ethics) dressed with a LENS label, when the anchor is mismatched, or when the lens-approach is generic ("governance matters," "risk management").

## 1. Summary — counts by rating

| Rating | Count | Share |
|--------|-------|-------|
| STRONG | 96 | 48% |
| SOLID | 78 | 39% |
| WEAK | 22 | 11% |
| POOR | 6 | 3% |

The corpus is, on the whole, a genuine capability-engineering collection: **86% (174/202) are STRONG or SOLID.** The weak tail is concentrated and patterned (see §4), not scattered. The single most common weakness is *materials/structural/process-engineering failures from the v1 catastrophe set* whose actual lesson is a design-calculation or fraud lesson, with the human-capability framing carried by a generic governance anchor. The poorest fits are a handful of pure financial-fraud, pure data-breach, and pure structural-collapse cases where the capability dimension is real but peripheral, and one closing essay that is framing rather than a case.

The v2 corpus (101–201) is, somewhat counterintuitively, a *tighter* fit than the v1 tail — most v2 cases were drafted to a named CLO and an explicit measurement/teaching point, so their lens-approach is rarely generic. The weak fits in v2 are a different problem: a small cluster of reflective-practice "genre exemplar" cases (174–176, 188) that are about a writing genre rather than a capability intervention, and a cluster of higher-ed finance/disclosure cases (177–180, 189) that lean toward policy/legal analysis.

## 4. Patterns

**P1 — The v1 catastrophe set's pure-engineering cases are the weakest fits.** Every POOR case except the closing essay (51 Pinto, 73 Equifax, 75 FIU, 81 Tacoma, 85 Madoff) comes from the v1 "big disaster" corpus, and each is really a *materials-failure, calculation-error, fraud, or cyber-ops* case wearing a generic governance/knowledge-transfer label. This is exactly the catastrophe-bias the preface to competencies.md names: the cases with the richest public record are over-represented, and some of them got in on magnitude rather than on a clean capability-engineering lesson. The fix is consistent — either cut, or invert the narrative so the (real but secondary) learning-engineering thread leads.

**P2 — A utility/infrastructure trio is redundant.** 76 (Camp Fire), 77 (Texas Grid), 82 (Aliso Canyon) all teach the *same* 7.3 "legacy asset aged past its oversight regime" lesson with thin human-capability content. Three near-identical cases dilute the point; one taught cluster would be stronger.

**P3 — A financial-fraud cluster stretches the "measurement" framing.** 71 (VW), 83 (LIBOR), 84 (APS), 85 (Madoff), with 70 (Wells Fargo) as the anchor, are all tagged to C2 measurement-gaming. Wells Fargo and UBB (60) genuinely teach incentive/instrument design; the others teach *that fraud occurred* and assert the design lesson without developing it. The competency is real (2.2 is well-founded) but it is over-subscribed with scandal narratives that don't carry a constructive capability lesson.

**P4 — The v2 reflective-practice cluster (174–176, 188) is about a genre, not a capability.** These were drafted as "prior art" for editor-commissioned Practice Flywheel accounts (per the dossier's own notes and CLAUDE.md task B1). They are method/genre exemplars and read as such. Recommend collapsing them into one method-note companion to the first-person accounts rather than four numbered cases — otherwise a reflective-writing genre is over-weighted relative to the engineering discipline.

**P5 — The frontier human-AI cases split cleanly into "designed" and "topic."** The strong frontier cases name a *specific designed pattern* — TREWS/Epic/COMPOSER (delegation-with-revocation, validation-before-trust), SyRI/Waymo/CPUC (objection-dissolving artifacts), Petrov (human as recoverability). The weak ones (96 ChatGPT-in-healthcare, 99 AI-coding) name a *phenomenon* and gesture at "verification at point of use" without a designed artifact. The discriminator is whether the lens-approach survives the test "what would a learning engineer *build* here?" — when it can't answer, the case is a topic.

**P6 — v2 is, on balance, the tighter corpus.** Counterintuitive but clear: because v2 cases were drafted to a named CLO with an explicit measurement/teaching point, their lens-approach is rarely generic. The weak v2 cases fail for *narrowness* (genre, or policy/legal analysis: 177–180, 189 lean toward higher-ed-finance disclosure law) rather than for the v1 failure of *generic-label-on-a-non-LE-case*. The single best lever for raising the corpus's average fit is editing the ~12 v1 catastrophe stragglers, not the v2 set.

**P7 — The dual-anchor convention occasionally over-applies the AI CLOs.** "Delegation with revocation" and "machine teaming" (D5) are being attached to cases with no machine in them (117 iPLEDGE, partly 192 Robodebt's D5 tag). The CLO is sound; it is being stretched to non-AI authorization mechanisms because the language ("delegate authority, retain the right to revoke") generalizes. Worth a pass to confirm D5/PT6 tags actually involve an automated agent.

## 5. Method note

I read all 202 case entries in the dossier against the introduction's six tenets and five competencies, competencies.md's induced 8/32, and the canonical five-competency/sub-objective list. Each case was scored on the two-part test (genuine capability-engineering lesson + non-stretch anchor + specific, non-generic lens-approach). I deliberately held the WEAK line at "the LENS connection is real but strained or the anchor is mismatched or the lens-approach is generic," and reserved POOR for cases that do not teach a capability-engineering lesson at all. Where I judged a case borderline, I named it in §2 so the editor can re-draw the line. No case files were modified.
