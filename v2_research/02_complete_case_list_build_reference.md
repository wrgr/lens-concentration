### v2-805 — The Kirkpatrick Chain of Evidence and Where Corporate L&D Stops
- year: 1959 (model); ongoing critique
- tier: big | kind: frontier
- induced anchor: 2.1 — Measuring the failure mode you care about
- LENS: Domain 3 (Measurement); Problem Type 5
- citation: Kirkpatrick four-level model (Reaction/Learning/Behavior/Results, 1959, "chain of evidence"); critique synthesized across L&D evaluation literature (verified: Devlin Peck, D2L, Valamis evaluation guides documenting that most teams stop at Levels 1–2)
- domains: corporate L&D, training evaluation, workforce development
- capability question: Does corporate training actually change on-the-job behavior and business results, and can the organization even tell?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: Kirkpatrick's four levels (Reaction → Learning → Behavior → Results) are the dominant corporate-training evaluation framework, increasingly framed as a "chain of evidence." The documented systemic failure: most organizations stop at Levels 1–2 (satisfaction and knowledge) because Level 3 (behavior change) and Level 4 (business results) require data they cannot access — and satisfaction scores do not predict performance outcomes, nor does knowledge retention guarantee workplace application. The result is a field that spends heavily (US organizations reported >$125B/year on training per the ASTD figure cited in Blume et al.) while measuring mostly the variable that flatters the program. This is the corporate-scale instance of the enthusiasm-evidence gap and a direct illustration of the revised "decision-grade evidence" point: the evidence most L&D decisions ride on is structurally sub-decision-grade.
- why this fits: It is the measurement-self-deception pattern at corporate scale, and the workforce-L&D case the corpus most needed for dual-audience balance.
- editor note: Frontier/synthesis case, not a single-study case. Anchor to the model and the documented stop-at-L2 pattern; the secondary sources are evaluation-practice guides, so frame as "well-documented practice pattern," not a single peer-reviewed finding. Pairs with v2-806.

### v2-806 — Training Transfer: The Gap Between Learning and Doing
- year: 2010
- tier: big | kind: frontier (evidence-gap with positive predictors)
- induced anchor: 2.3 — Closed-loop evidence linked to intervention
- LENS: Domain 2 (Learning Engineering Design); Problem Type 4
- citation: Blume, Ford, Baldwin & Huang, "Transfer of Training: A Meta-Analytic Review," Journal of Management 2010, 36(4):1065–1105, DOI 10.1177/0149206309352880 (verified, fetched)
- domains: corporate L&D, training transfer, organizational behavior
- capability question: Does what is learned in training transfer to changed performance on the job, and what predicts whether it does?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: A meta-analysis of 89 empirical studies found that transfer of training — the extent to which learning leads to meaningful changes in work performance — is positively related to cognitive ability, conscientiousness, motivation, and crucially a *supportive work environment* (with superior support among the strongest predictors). But the authors are explicit that the literature is "characterized by inconsistent measurement of transfer and significant variability in findings," and downstream summaries note organizations often see limited return because learning fails to transfer. The case teaches that the capability deliverable is not the training event but the transfer, and that the work environment — not just the learner or the course — determines whether capability actually appears on the job. This is the "the human is the biggest variable at the interface" claim, with the interface being the return-to-work boundary.
- why this fits: It links the intervention (training) to the outcome (job performance) through a measured, replicated evidence base, and locates the decisive variable at the system/environment level rather than the individual — core LENS framing.
- editor note: The strongest single corporate-L&D citation in the set (top-tier journal, large meta-analysis). Reproduce the "inconsistent measurement / significant variability" hedge — it is load-bearing and reinforces the evidence-architecture theme.
