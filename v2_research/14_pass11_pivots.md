## Pivot 2 — Wisconsin DPI Dropout Early Warning System (DEWS)

The DEWS deployment is the structural gap the v2 corpus most needs: a K–12 predictive-fairness deployment with **two layers of evidence** — peer-reviewed academic analysis (Perdomo et al., FAccT 2025) of the causal-effect-on-graduation question, and investigation-grade journalism (Feathers / The Markup, 2023) on the demographic-disparity question — *plus* Wisconsin DPI's own 2021 internal equity audit conceding the model is not fair. The case carries hedges at three layers (academic causal-inference, journalistic statistical reconstruction, agency self-disclosure), and the deployment is still live as of mid-2026. Two candidates below: one academic-tier, one journalism-tier; the editor may decide to merge them into a single case with both anchors, or keep two parallel entries the way v2-501 / v2-929 split the Open University story.

### v2-934 — Wisconsin DEWS Causal Evaluation: "Difficult Lessons on Social Prediction from Wisconsin Public Schools" (Perdomo, Britton, Hardt, Abebe — arXiv 2023 / FAccT 2025)
- year: 2023 (arXiv preprint); 2025 (FAccT peer-reviewed publication)
- scale: big (statewide deployment since 2012; ~10 years of state-administrative data; >32,000 students labeled "high risk" in 2020–21 alone)
- proposed-kind: failure (deployed predictive system functioning largely as designed yet producing no measurable improvement in the outcome it was deployed to improve)
- proposed-anchor (induced): 8.3 — Designing predictions to trigger support, not gatekeeping (the failure is at the support-half: the prediction was made, but the closed loop to the intervention path was either absent or ineffective)
- alternate-anchors: 8.2 (Demographic stratification of validation and outcomes — the regression-discontinuity stratification surfaces what aggregate validation hid); 2.3 (Closed-loop evidence linked to intervention — the closed loop was *missing*); 8.1 (Choosing the construct — what counts as "at-risk" and what counts as "improvement")
- LENS mapping: Domain 3 (Measurement); Domain 4 (Context / sociotechnical constraints); Problem Type 5 (Evidence under operational constraint); secondary Problem Type 4 (Evidence-based design at scale)
- CLO anchor: CLO-3; [NEW] CLO — Gap attribution; [NEW] CLO — Judgment under inadequate evidence; [NEW] CLO — Fairness beyond omission
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed (FAccT 2025 proceedings)
- evidence-flag: *(none — peer-reviewed at FAccT, the central venue for algorithmic-fairness deployed evaluations)*
- citation: Perdomo, J. C., Britton, T., Hardt, M., & Abebe, R., "Difficult Lessons on Social Prediction from Wisconsin Public Schools," *Proceedings of the 2025 ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency* (FAccT '25), 2025, DOI 10.1145/3715275.3732175; preprint arXiv 2304.06205 (April 2023, revised September 2023), https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.06205
- domains: K-12 education, predictive analytics, algorithmic fairness
- capability question: When a state education agency deploys a predictive early-warning system for nearly a decade and the largest available causal study cannot rule out a zero treatment effect on graduation, what has the deployment actually been doing — and what evidence did the agency owe itself before scale-up?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: The authors use ten years of Wisconsin DEWS administrative data — the longest available evaluation horizon for any U.S. K–12 early-warning system — to ask whether DEWS *causally* improved graduation outcomes for the students it labeled high-risk. Using a regression-discontinuity design around the 78.5 risk-score threshold (students just below vs. just above), they find that DEWS "accurately sorts students by their dropout risk" — the accuracy claim survives — but the data "cannot reliably rule out that there has been no positive treatment effect" on graduation. They also propose, as a constructive alternative, that environment-level information (school, district) can target interventions about as efficiently as individual risk scores, raising the question of whether individual prediction was ever the load-bearing variable. **Author-stated hedges (preserve verbatim):** (a) the RD design bounds the causal claim to students near the threshold and does not directly identify effects on the most-at-risk students; (b) the analysis cannot separate the effect of *the prediction itself* from the effect of *whatever district-level interventions districts deployed in response to the prediction*, which varied; (c) Wisconsin DPI's own equity analysis (2021) is acknowledged as a load-bearing context input but is not independently replicated in the paper. Future validation ongoing on whether the post-2023 DPI changes (under sustained external pressure following the Markup investigation) materially change the causal picture.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: 8.3 is the support-vs-gatekeeping anchor and DEWS is its most carefully measured failure mode at K–12 scale — the system was nominally built to *trigger* support, but the closed loop to the support intervention was not engineered with enough specificity to produce a measurable graduation effect. This is the inverse of Case 39 (Georgia State, the positive existence proof). The 8.2 alternate carries the stratification finding the Markup investigation makes inescapable (see v2-935). The "judgment under inadequate evidence" CLO is directly motivated: the agency kept deploying for nine years on accuracy evidence, never on causal evidence.
- which pivot: 2 (Wisconsin DEWS — academic-tier anchor)
- pairing recommendation: paired-first with v2-935 (the journalism-tier disparate-impact reconstruction) and v1 Case 37 (Educational Predictive Analytics) and Case 39 (Georgia State). The pairing teaches the *two halves* of the predictive-EWS question — does it predict accurately (yes), and does the prediction-plus-intervention loop produce the outcome (the largest causal evaluation cannot show it does).

### v2-935 — Wisconsin DEWS Disparate Impact and DPI Self-Audit (The Markup investigation — Feathers, 2023; Wisconsin DPI 2021 internal equity analysis)
- year: 2021 (DPI internal equity audit); 2023 (Markup investigation publication)
- scale: big (statewide deployment since 2012; ~15% of all Wisconsin 6th–9th graders labeled "high risk" in 2020–21 — over 32,000 students)
- proposed-kind: failure
- proposed-anchor (induced): 8.2 — Demographic stratification of validation and outcomes
- alternate-anchors: 8.4 (Surfacing bias through governance, not just technique — the DPI audit conceded the fairness failure in 2021 but the deployment continued); 5.4 (Change-control and disclosure as governance artifacts — the DPI presentation slide titled "Is DEWS Fair?" answered "no…" internally without an external disclosure event); 8.1 (Choosing the construct — race and free/reduced-lunch status as model inputs is itself a construct decision)
- LENS mapping: Domain 3 (Measurement) + Domain 4 (Context / sociotechnical); Problem Type 5 (Evidence under operational constraint); secondary Problem Type 6
- CLO anchor: CLO-3; CLO-4; [NEW] CLO — Fairness beyond omission; [NEW] CLO — Gap attribution
- evidence-source: journalism (The Markup investigation, co-published with Chalkbeat) + program-report (Wisconsin DPI's own 2021 equity-analysis presentation, obtained via the investigation)
- evidence-flag: **journalism-tier** — the headline statistical claims are the Markup's reconstruction from DPI-provided records and FOIA-style requests; the DPI's own 2021 audit is the agency's own document and is investigation-grade for the agency-self-disclosure half; future validation ongoing
- citation: Feathers, T., "False Alarm: How Wisconsin Uses Race and Income to Label Students 'High Risk,'" *The Markup*, 27 April 2023, https://themarkup.org/machine-learning/2023/04/27/false-alarm-how-wisconsin-uses-race-and-income-to-label-students-high-risk (co-published Chalkbeat, https://www.chalkbeat.org/2023/4/27/23699361/dropout-early-warning-system-dews-student-dropouts-race-income-data/); Wisconsin Department of Public Instruction, internal equity analysis of DEWS (2021), as quoted and reproduced in the Markup investigation; Wisconsin DPI DEWS page, https://dpi.wi.gov/ews/dropout
- domains: K-12 education, algorithmic fairness, public-sector predictive analytics
- capability question: When a state education agency's own 2021 equity audit concludes that its deployed predictive model "over-identifies white students among on-time graduates while it over-identifies Black, Hispanic and other students of color among non-on-time graduates," and the agency continues to deploy the model unchanged for two more years until external journalism surfaces the audit, what does the agency owe the affected population — and on what timeline?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: The Markup's reconstruction documents that DEWS, deployed by Wisconsin DPI since 2012, uses race and free/reduced-lunch status as direct inputs to its 0–100 risk score, with a 78.5 threshold for "high-risk" labeling. Headline statistical findings (Markup reconstruction): the false-alarm rate is 42 percentage points higher for Black students than for White students and 18 percentage points higher for Hispanic students; when DEWS predicts non-graduation, it is wrong 74% of the time overall; in 2018 Kenosha, 43% of Black 9th-graders were labeled high-risk vs. 11% of White 9th-graders. **The agency-self-disclosure half (load-bearing, preserve verbatim):** Wisconsin DPI's own 2021 internal equity analysis concluded that "the model over-identifies white students among on-time graduates while it over-identifies Black, Hispanic and other students of color among non-on-time graduates," and the agency presentation slide titled "Is DEWS Fair?" answered internally with "no…". The agency continued deployment unchanged. DPI's public response to the Markup investigation, via spokesperson Abigail Swetz, was that "the data analysis isn't racist. It's math that reflects our systems" — a quote that itself becomes a load-bearing teaching artifact about how agencies frame algorithmic fairness as upstream-causation rather than as deliverable design. **Hedges:** (a) the Markup's effect-size figures rest on its reconstruction of DPI data and on the DPI audit as primary source; an independent replication of the disparate-impact figures has not been published in a peer-reviewed venue beyond what v2-934 (Perdomo et al.) carries on the causal-effect half; (b) the post-2023 DPI response, including whether and how the model has been recalibrated, is not yet documented in primary published sources at investigation grade. Future validation ongoing.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: 8.2 is demographic-stratified validation. DEWS is the rare deployment where the *agency's own internal stratification audit* (2021) reached the right conclusion and the agency continued deploying anyway — the governance failure (8.4 alternate) is the load-bearing teaching. Pairs with Case 35 (UK A-Level Algorithm) and Case 97 (Predictive Policing) as the K–12 sibling of the public-sector predictive-fairness trio; pairs with v2-934 as the causal-effect counterpart; pairs with v2-501 / v2-929 (Open University governance + teacher-use) as the *positive*-governance counterexample where the agency *did* publish the ethical-use framework before scale-up.
- which pivot: 2 (Wisconsin DEWS — journalism-tier disparate-impact + agency-self-disclosure anchor)
- coi: none
- pairing recommendation: editor's call between (a) merging v2-934 and v2-935 into a single Wisconsin DEWS case with both anchors (causal-effect + disparate-impact + agency self-audit) carried in one extended case, or (b) keeping two parallel cases the way v2-501 / v2-929 split Open University into a governance frame and a teacher-use frame. Option (a) reads cleaner as a single teaching unit; option (b) preserves the evidence-tier separation (peer-reviewed vs. journalism-tier) and lets the v2-935 `evidence-flag: journalism-tier` render under the title without contaminating the v2-934 academic-tier reading.
