## Multidisciplinary translation at table-stakes scale — education and healthcare

These are the cases the editor flagged as "there if you look": efforts where the core difficulty is *translation across disciplines* and the evidence sits at program/study scale. They share a structural feature the casebook should name — the capability being engineered is the collaboration itself, and the honest finding across all of them is that the enthusiasm for interdisciplinary work runs ahead of the measured evidence.

### Team Science Training for Clinical & Translational Scientists
- proposed-id: v2-403
- year: 2020–2022
- scale: small
- proposed-kind: intervention
- proposed-anchor: 4.3 — Authority gradient and team-coordination redesign
- alternate-anchors: [1.3 — Cross-organization and joint requirements; 2.1 — Measuring the failure mode you care about]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: "Team science training for clinical and translational scientists: An assessment of effectiveness," J Clin Transl Sci, PMC12392353 (verified)
- domains: clinical/translational research, team science, healthcare workforce
- capability question: Can researchers from different disciplines be trained to collaborate effectively on translational problems, and can that capability be measured?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: The Colorado Clinical and Translational Sciences Institute built a structured team-science training program and evaluated it 2020–2022 with pre/post surveys (N=221), introducing a validated instrument (TEAMS) that resolved into three competency components — Team Planning, Managing a Team, and Interpersonal Relations. Participants showed statistically significant improvement (p<0.05) in all three, with the largest gains in the structured/tool-based domains and smaller gains in Interpersonal Relations. The honest limit, stated by the authors: outcomes are self-reported and the design lacks longitudinal tracking and integration metrics, so this is evidence of perceived competency gain, not yet demonstrated downstream research-collaboration impact.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: It treats interdisciplinary collaboration as an engineerable, measurable capability and redesigns team-coordination practices — and it is honest that the interpersonal half (the hardest part) moved least.

### Interprofessional Education (IPE) and the Evidence Gap
- proposed-id: v2-404
- year: 2013–2015
- scale: big
- proposed-kind: frontier
- proposed-anchor: 2.1 — Measuring the failure mode you care about
- alternate-anchors: [4.3 — Authority gradient and team-coordination redesign; 6.4 — Cross-domain adaptation]
- evidence-source: investigation
- citation: Reeves et al., Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2013, DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD002213.pub3 (verified); IOM/National Academies, "Measuring the Impact of Interprofessional Education on Collaborative Practice and Patient Outcomes," 2015, NCBI NBK338352 (verified)
- domains: health professions education, interprofessional collaboration, patient safety
- capability question: Does educating health professionals together (rather than in disciplinary silos) actually improve collaboration and patient outcomes?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: IPE is a decades-long, well-funded movement premised on the idea that doctors, nurses, pharmacists, and allied professionals should learn together to collaborate better. The 2013 Cochrane review found only 15 studies between 1999 and 2011 met its inclusion criteria, and while those showed some positive outcomes, the evidence base for linking IPE to measurable changes in practice and patient outcomes was thin. The 2015 IOM report made the gap its central finding: there remains a "paucity of high-quality research" connecting IPE interventions to patient outcomes, and it proposed a conceptual model for doing the measurement properly. This is a frontier/measurement case: a large, sincere, multidisciplinary translation effort whose core problem is that the field cannot yet measure whether it works.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: The capability question (does interdisciplinary education improve care?) is real, but the documented failure is one of evidence architecture — the field instrumented enthusiasm faster than it instrumented outcomes.
- (frontier note) Points to a recurring competency the framework may want to name explicitly: **measuring the capability of a collaboration itself**, as distinct from the capability of any individual operator. Team science (v2-403) and IPE are two instances; CRM (v1) is a third. This may be a sub-competency bridging C2 and C4.

### Implementation Science Training — Teaching the Translation Itself
- proposed-id: v2-405
- year: 2020s
- scale: small
- proposed-kind: frontier
- proposed-anchor: 6.4 — Cross-domain adaptation of proven interventions
- alternate-anchors: [2.3 — Closed-loop evidence linked to intervention]
- evidence-source: peer-reviewed
- citation: CTSA T32/TL1 program-goals study, J Clin Transl Sci 2021, PMC8826009 (verified)
- domains: implementation science, translational workforce, education
- capability question: Can the discipline of moving evidence into practice (implementation science) itself be taught systematically across institutions?
- what happened / what worked / what's open: A survey of CTSA-funded TL1 training programs (N=50) found that while most named collaboration, team science, and multi/inter/cross-disciplinary training as goals, far fewer (14–24%) actually embedded competency-based training and assessment, program evaluation, or experiential learning. The picture is of a field that has converged on what interdisciplinary translation training should aim for, but where the operational practices (assessment, evaluation) lag the stated goals — the same enthusiasm-ahead-of-evidence pattern as IPE. This connects directly to the v1 "implementation science 17-year gap" case (Case 41) as its workforce-training counterpart.
- why this fits the proposed anchor: It is about institutionalizing the cross-domain transfer of proven interventions as a trainable discipline, and documents the gap between stated and operational training requirements.
- deeper-evidence-of-v1: Workforce-training counterpart to Case 41 (implementation science 17-year gap). Distinct angle, cross-reference.

## What these cases add to the balance picture (updating pass 4)
1. **They populate the education-healthcare *translation* boundary**, which the v1 catastrophe canon largely skips — the daily, unglamorous work of getting disciplines to collaborate, measured honestly.
2. **They surface a candidate new sub-competency:** *measuring the capability of a collaboration itself*, separate from individual-operator capability (v2-403, v2-404, plus v1 CRM and TeamSTEPPS). Worth raising with the framework owners; it threads C2 and C4 and is exactly the kind of "the human is the biggest variable at the interface" claim the LENS framing rests on.
3. **They are honest about the enthusiasm-evidence gap** — every one of them found the measured effect thinner than the field's confidence. That is itself a load-bearing lesson for a learning-engineering casebook and pairs well with the C2 evidence-architecture competency.
4. **CIRCUIT correction matters for the COI posture:** the case is evidentially sound (peer-reviewed, DOI); the right move is transparent disclosure that an editor co-authored the study, not exclusion. Hiding a sound case would be worse for credibility than disclosing a sound one.

## Caveats
- v2-403 and v2-405 are self-reported / survey-based program evaluations; v2-404 is explicitly a documented evidence *gap*, not a success. None should be drafted as a clean intervention win — their value is in modeling honest measurement of hard-to-measure collaborative capability.
- The CIRCUIT case (v2-402a) is editor-authored; the disclosure recommendation is not optional if the book wants to keep its evidentiary credibility intact.
